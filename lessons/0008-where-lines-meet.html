Where Lines Meet 💥

Two lines on one grid — sooner or later most pairs collide. Today you become the crash investigator.

🎯 In this lesson

You’ll find the intersection of two lines — the single point (x, y) that obeys both rules at once. First by reading it straight off the graph, then by a slick bit of algebra that needs no graph at all. And you’ll learn to spot the one kind of pair that never meets. 👯

Two rules, one point

Remember: every line is a rule. Here are two rules sharing one grid — y = 2x − 1 in purple and y = −x + 5 in pink.

The amber point (2, 3) is where they crash — and it’s special: it’s the only point in the universe that obeys both rules. Check it yourself:

  • Purple rule: y = 2 × 2 − 1 = 3 ✓
  • Pink rule: y = −2 + 5 = 3 ✓

Same x, same y, both equations happy. Every other point on the grid disappoints at least one of them.

The algebra shortcut

No graph handy? No problem. At the crash point the two lines have the same y — so their right-hand sides must be equal. Set mx + c = m′x + c′, solve for x, then substitute back to get y. Watch it work on the same pair:

  1. Set the right-hand sides equal: 2x − 1 = −x + 5
  2. Add x to both sides: 3x − 1 = 5
  3. Add 1 to both sides: 3x = 6, so x = 2
  4. Substitute into either line: y = 2 × 2 − 1 = 3
Crash site: (2, 3) 💥

Exactly what the graph said — but this way works even when the answer isn’t a nice whole number, and it’s the method examiners love to see.

Lines that never meet 👯

One famous exception. If two lines have the same gradient but different intercepts, they climb at exactly the same rate forever — so the gap between them never closes. They’re parallel: zero intersections.

y = 2x + 1 and y = 2x − 3: both have m = 2, so no crash, ever. (Try the algebra: 2x + 1 = 2x − 3 collapses to 1 = −3 — nonsense, which is algebra’s way of saying “never”.)

And one cheeky footnote: same gradient and same intercept means it’s the same line wearing two name tags — infinitely many shared points.
