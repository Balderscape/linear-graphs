Undercover Lines: Rearranging 🥸

Not every line politely introduces itself as y = mx + c. Some arrive in disguise. Your job: unmask them.

🎯 In this lesson

Equations like 2y = 6x + 8, y + 2x = 7 and 2x + 3y = 12 are all straight lines — they’re just y = mx + c wearing a fake moustache. By the end you’ll rearrange any of them so that y is alone on the left — and only then read off m and c. Reading them off too early is the #1 exam trap. Not you, though. 😏

The golden rule ⚖️

An equation is a set of scales: whatever you do to one side, do to the other, and it stays balanced. Your mission never changes: get y alone on the left. Everything else follows from that one idea.

Example 1 — something stuck to the y? Move it across.

  • y − 4 = 3x  →  add 4 to both sides  →  y = 3x + 4
  • Now read off: m = 3, c = 4. ✅

Example 2 — a number multiplying the y? Divide it away.

  • 2y = 6x + 8  →  divide everything by 2  →  y = 3x + 4
  • Same line as Example 1, in a different disguise! m = 3, c = 4.
⚠️ The classic mistake: dividing only one term. 2y = 6x + 8 does not become y = 3x + 8 — the 8 must be divided by 2 as well. Every. Single. Term. If you halve one side of the scales, you halve all of the other side too.

Example 3 — an x-term hanging around on the left? Subtract it off.

  • y + 2x = 7  →  subtract 2x from both sides  →  y = −2x + 7
  • Read off: m = −2, c = 7.

And if you wrote y = 7 − 2x instead? Also correct! It’s the exact same line — the terms are just written in a different order. Neither version is “wrong”; m = −2 either way.

🕵️ The order trap: in y = 7 − 2x, the gradient is m = −2 and the intercept is c = 7. The order the terms are written in doesn’t matter one bit — the number glued to the x is always m (sign included!), and the lonely number is always c.

🥸 Unmask the line

Hit the button and I’ll deal you a random line in disguise. Before you click “Next step”, try to predict the unmasked y = mx + c in your head — then reveal and check.

Try these missions:
  1. Deal until you get a 2y = … or 3y = … disguise. Predict the answer, then check the constant got divided too.
  2. Deal until you get a y + □x = … one. Which way does the sign of m flip?
  3. Get three predictions right in a row before revealing. That’s unmasking mastery. 🏆
💚 Easy win: every single disguise here is beaten by one move — add, subtract, divide or multiply both whole sides. If you can spot which move, the exam question is already dead.
